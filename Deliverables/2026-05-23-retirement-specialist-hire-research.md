## What the best-in-world version actually does, day to day

A world-class Retirement Specialist is half mathematician, half therapist. Their job is to turn the complexity of Social Security, Medicare, and withdrawal timing into clear options that show lifetime impact.

**Core daily work:**

1. **Social Security strategy modeling** — pulls statements (all household members), inputs current age, FRA, earnings history, calculates present value of claiming at 62, 66, 67, 70. Models the full range. For couples, optimizes household lifetime benefits, not individual benefits. Special attention to spousal benefits (critical for homemakers or low-earning spouses) and earnings test impact for those still working.

2. **Medicare enrollment planning** — reviews turning-65 deadlines (three months before/after), evaluates Part A (Hospital Insurance, usually free), Part B (Medical Insurance, premium), Part D (Prescription Drug), and Medigap vs Medicare Advantage trade-offs. Connects Medicare plan selection to IRMAA (Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount) thresholds — premium taxes kick in at $194k provisional income for married couples (2024). Many retirees miss this link.

3. **Withdrawal sequencing** — designs a 20-30 year strategy pulling from 401k, Traditional IRA, Roth IRA, and taxable accounts in order that minimizes taxes and IRMAA while maintaining cash flow. Coordinates with Social Security claiming: if claiming at 67, withdraw more from 401k at 66; if delaying to 70, hold off on Traditional IRA withdrawals to manage provisional income.

4. **Spousal dependency risk** — plans for the case where one spouse dies. What does the surviving spouse's income look like? Are they exposed? Does the claiming strategy protect them?

5. **Healthcare transition** — if moving from company plan to Medicare, models the bridge gap (age 65 to 70?), costs, and plan selection.

## Core competencies vs anti-patterns

### What world-class output looks like

- **Scenario spreadsheets**: Comparing claiming at 62, 66, 67, 70 with lifetime cumulative benefit totals, monthly income, and present-value calculations. Client can audit the math.
- **Household optimization**: For couples, showing that one spouse claiming at 62 while the other delays to 70 often beats both claiming at 67. Explains the strategy, not just the result.
- **IRMAA awareness**: Flagging "if you claim Social Security at 67 and withdraw $30k from 401k, you hit the first IRMAA bracket—Medicare costs jump $70/month." Quantifies the trade-off.
- **Earnings test clarity**: "You're still working at $105k/year. Benefits will be withheld, but the Social Security Administration will recalculate at your FRA. This is a temporary withholding, not a permanent loss."
- **Medicare plan comparison matrix**: Showing premium, copays, deductible, out-of-pocket max for Medigap vs Medicare Advantage options side-by-side.
- **Withdrawal sequencing timeline**: Year-by-year distribution amounts from each account, tied to Social Security claiming and tax brackets.

### Anti-patterns to avoid

1. **"Delay until 70" without modeling.** Generic advice that ignores the client's specific longevity, earnings, spousal situation. Some people have good reasons to claim earlier.

2. **Ignoring earnings test for those still working.** A 67-year-old working full-time at $105k and claiming Social Security will have benefits withheld. Failing to model this creates unrealistic cash flow expectations.

3. **Treating each spouse independently.** Social Security is fundamentally a household optimization problem. Spouses have filing options (file and suspend, restricted application in some cases, deemed filing rules). Failing to model the couple's combined lifetime benefits leaves thousands on the table.

4. **Forgetting IRMAA is part of Social Security planning.** If claiming triggers IRMAA, Medicare Part B and Part D premiums jump 85–560% depending on income level. This cost must be factored into the claiming decision.

5. **No withdrawal sequencing strategy.** Client reaches retirement and withdraws randomly, creating unnecessary tax and IRMAA burden. Good specialists build a 20+ year sequence.

6. **Ignoring Roth conversion windows.** In low-income years (e.g., 65–66, before Social Security and RMDs kick in), retirees often have room to do a Roth conversion at low tax cost. Specialists who miss this leave optimization on the table.

7. **Not flagging Medicare enrollment deadlines.** Missing the window around 65 results in permanent Late Enrollment Penalties. Specialists must track dates and alert clients to deadlines.

8. **Healthcare gap blindness.** Planning assumes Medicare at 65, but if retiring at 62, there's a 3-year gap. Specialists must cost out the bridge (COBRA, ACA, spouse's plan, retiree coverage).

## Deliverables and world-class output

A complete retirement income strategy package includes:

1. **Claiming Strategy Memo** (1–2 pages) — narrative summary of recommendation, key decision points, household lifetime benefits comparison, monthly income in retirement.

2. **Scenario Comparison Spreadsheet** — Excel or CSV with claiming age options, lifetime benefits, tax impact, monthly income, IRMAA implications.

3. **Medicare Planning Summary** (1–2 pages) — enrollment timeline (deadlines marked), plan comparison matrix, cost estimates for Medigap vs Advantage, prescription drug plan selection.

4. **Withdrawal Sequencing Plan** (spreadsheet or narrative) — year-by-year account allocation (401k, IRA, taxable), tax basis tracking, RMD calculations.

5. **Risk Sensitivity Analysis** — longevity scenarios (live to 80, 90, 100), market downturn scenarios, spousal life events. Shows plan robustness.

The best specialists provide 2–3 coherent scenarios (e.g., "claim at 67 with conservative withdrawals," "delay to 70 with Roth conversions," "claim at 62 with immediate withdrawal strategy") and let the client choose based on their values and goals, not the specialist's preference.

## Boundaries: what they refuse or hand back

- **Not tax specialists.** They model tax impact for IRMAA purposes, but real tax planning (capital gains harvesting, Roth conversions, state tax optimization) hands off to a CPA or tax planner.
- **Not investment advisors.** They do not recommend asset allocation, rebalancing, or specific securities.
- **Not Medicaid specialists.** They focus on Medicare for retirement-age beneficiaries. Medicaid planning (for long-term care, nursing homes) is outside scope.
- **Not CFP holistic planners.** They stay in their lane: retirement income. Estate planning, insurance needs, educational savings, debt payoff all go elsewhere.

## Market research: credentials, typical costs, qualifications

### Credentials market looks like

- **RFC (Retirement Income Certified)** — American College of Financial Services, 20-hour course, exam, $2,000–$5,000 cost. Recognized credential.
- **CFP (Certified Financial Planner)** — 6,000+ hours experience, rigorous exam, ongoing education. Top credential but often overkill for retirement-only focus.
- **ChFC (Chartered Financial Consultant)** — similar to CFP, often combined with Social Security specialization courses.
- **CFA (Chartered Financial Analyst)** — investment-focused, not required for retirement income planning.
- **Enrolled Agent (EA)** — IRS credential, useful for those who also handle tax planning.
- **Special certifications**: Social Security Claiming Strategies (many providers: AFSI, XO, etc.), Medicare Planning Specialist.

Best candidates often have:
- 8–15 years in financial planning or retirement income focus
- RFC, CFP, or ChFC
- Specialized training in Social Security and Medicare (beyond generic CFP)
- Track record of producing scenario models and education-focused deliverables

### Market costs

- **Comprehensive retirement income plan (full strategy)**: $2,000–$8,000 (20–40 hours). High-end ($8k) for complex couple situations.
- **Hourly consulting**: $150–$300/hour depending on credentials and market. CPAs and CFPs at high end.
- **Social Security claiming strategy only** (no full plan): $500–$2,000 for a streamlined analysis.

3. **IRMAA strategy**: "Can you explain your process for minimizing IRMAA? How do Social Security claiming and withdrawal sequencing interact with IRMAA thresholds?"

4. **Spousal benefits**: "A homemaker spouse has minimal work history. What's your approach to calculating spousal benefits vs their own retirement benefit, and which is larger?"

5. **Medicare coordination**: "How do you connect Medicare plan selection to Social Security claiming decisions? Give an example where plan choice changes because of IRMAA."

6. **Withdrawal sequencing**: "How do you build a 20-year withdrawal strategy from 401k, Traditional IRA, and taxable accounts? What role does tax basis play?"

7. **Roth conversion**: "In what scenarios do you model a Roth conversion, and how do you coordinate it with Social Security claiming and IRMAA?"

8. **References**: "Can you share a case study or example of a retirement income plan you produced? What was the client's biggest 'aha' moment?"
