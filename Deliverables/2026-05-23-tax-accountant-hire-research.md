## What the best-in-world version actually does, day to day

A world-class Tax Accountant in the retirement space is not just a tax return preparer. They are a strategist who sees taxes as a 20-year optimization problem, not a one-year filing. Their core job is to minimize lifetime tax burden by making sure every retirement income decision (Social Security claiming, Medicare timing, 401k withdrawals, Roth conversions, capital gains harvesting) is coordinated through a tax lens.

**Core daily work:**

1. **Multi-year tax projections** — models federal and state taxes over 10–20 years across different Social Security claiming scenarios. Shows ordinary income, capital gains, Social Security taxability, Medicare premiums (Part B, Part D), IRMAA taxes, and total tax burden year by year.

2. **IRMAA threshold mapping** — translates provisional income thresholds ($194k for married filing jointly in 2024) into actionable withdrawal and claiming decisions. Identifies which Social Security claiming age + withdrawal strategy keeps the client below IRMAA brackets.

3. **Capital gains harvesting** — audits current holdings, identifies unrealized gains and losses, models harvest opportunities year by year, tracks basis and carryforward losses to offset future gains.

4. **Roth conversion analysis** — identifies low-income years where a conversion to Roth IRA is tax-efficient (e.g., ages 65–66 before Social Security and RMDs kick in), models the tax cost and 20-year benefit, coordinates with Social Security claiming to avoid IRMAA triggers.

5. **Quarterly estimated taxes** — if applicable, builds a payment schedule to avoid penalties and underwithholding.

6. **Federal and state returns** — accurate preparation of Form 1040 (and state returns), correctly reporting Social Security taxability, Medicare premiums, investment income, charitable giving, capital gains.

7. **Relocation tax planning** — for moves between states, identifies tax implications (e.g., Iowa does NOT tax Social Security income; this is a huge planning advantage). Models how state change affects tax burden.

## Core competencies vs anti-patterns

### What world-class output looks like

- **Multi-year projection with trade-offs**: "If you claim Social Security at 67 and withdraw $30k/year from your 401k, your federal tax is $8,500/year and IRMAA costs you $70/month in Medicare premiums. If you delay to 70 and do a Roth conversion, federal tax jumps to $12,500 but IRMAA drops to $0 for three years, and your 20-year tax burden is $15,000 lower."

- **Provisional income tracking**: Breaks down provisional income (AGI + non-taxable interest + 1/2 Social Security) and shows which claiming/withdrawal combo stays below key thresholds.

- **Capital gains harvest plan**: Year-by-year chart showing realized gains, available losses to harvest, and recommended transactions. Client can see exactly which sales to execute.

- **Iowa tax advantage flagged**: "Iowa does not tax Social Security income—this alone saves you roughly $5,000–$8,000 per year in state taxes compared to other states. This favors delaying Social Security (more benefit, all untaxed in Iowa)."

- **Quarterly payment schedule**: If self-employed or high-income, provides due dates, amounts, and safe harbor guidance to avoid penalties.

- **Roth conversion window**: "Ages 65–66, before Social Security and RMDs, you have only $40k AGI. A $50k Roth conversion costs $9,000 in taxes but creates $75k tax-free growth over 20 years—highly efficient."

### Anti-patterns to avoid

1. **Treating Social Security as separate from tax planning.** Social Security benefit amount affects provisional income, which affects IRMAA, which affects Medicare premiums, which affects total retirement tax burden. Specialists who plan taxes without coordinating Social Security create suboptimal results.

2. **Ignoring IRMAA entirely.** Medicare premium surcharges (IRMAA) can be 85–560% of the base premium depending on provisional income. A $15k withdrawal choice that triggers IRMAA costs $840/year in higher premiums. This is not tax, but it's part of retirement cash flow optimization and must be in the tax plan.

3. **Single-year tax focus.** Filing a 2026 return without modeling 2027–2045 tax burden. Example: a Roth conversion that costs $15k in 2026 taxes but saves $40k over 20 years is smart, but only a multi-year analysis sees this.

4. **No capital gains strategy.** Client has $200k in appreciated stock. Specialist prepares return but never mentions harvesting opportunities, realizing losses, or timing sales for tax efficiency. Leaves thousands on the table annually.

5. **Roth conversion blindness.** Opportunity years exist (low-income years, gap years between jobs, pre-RMD years). Specialists who don't model conversions miss easy 20-year tax savings.

6. **State tax arbitrage miss.** Iowa doesn't tax Social Security. A specialist who doesn't flag this advantage in the context of claiming strategy misses a major lever.

7. **No quarterly planning.** Client with $100k+ Social Security income + 401k distributions doesn't receive estimated tax guidance, triggers underwithholding penalties, files return with late balance due. Reactive, not proactive.

8. **401k/IRA distribution sequencing ignored.** RMDs start at 73 (SECURE Act 2.0). Specialists who don't coordinate RMD timing with Social Security claiming and withdrawal strategy create unnecessary tax clustering in later years.

## Deliverables and world-class output

A complete retirement tax strategy package includes:

1. **Multi-Year Tax Projection** (Excel or PDF) — 10–20 year federal and state tax forecast, showing each scenario (claim at 67, claim at 70, Roth conversion, etc.) with year-by-year tax burden and cumulative total.

2. **IRMAA Threshold Analysis** (1–2 page memo) — provisional income thresholds, which claiming/withdrawal combo stays below IRMAA brackets, cost of each bracket jump (Medicare premium impact).

3. **Capital Gains Harvest Plan** (spreadsheet) — current holdings with unrealized gains/losses, year-by-year harvest schedule, basis tracking, loss carryforward management.

4. **Roth Conversion Analysis** (memo + spreadsheet) — identified conversion opportunity years, conversion amount per year, tax cost, 20-year benefit analysis, coordination with Social Security claiming.

5. **Quarterly Estimated Tax Schedule** (spreadsheet) — payment amounts and due dates (April 15, June 15, Sept 15, Jan 15) with safe harbor analysis.

6. **Federal and State Tax Returns** — annual Form 1040, Schedule A/B/D (if applicable), Form 8960 (if NIIT applies), state return accurately reporting Social Security, investment income, and all applicable credits/deductions.

7. **Tax Planning Memo** (summary) — key findings, recommended strategies, timeline, and action items for the client.

The best specialists provide a visual dashboard or summary page showing: "Your 20-year tax burden under scenario A is $150,000. Under scenario B (with Roth conversions and harvesting) it's $135,000. Here's why." They connect tax decisions to retirement outcomes (lifestyle, healthcare, legacy) so clients understand the "why," not just the "what."

## Market research: credentials, typical costs, qualifications

### Credentials market looks like

- **CPA (Certified Public Accountant)** — required for tax return preparation and professional credibility. 150-hour education, exam, state license. Market entry.
- **Enrolled Agent (EA)** — IRS credential allowing tax practice without CPA. Easier exam than CPA but lower market credibility.
- **CFP (Certified Financial Planner)** — useful but not required. Adds credibility for holistic retirement planning.
- **CFA (Chartered Financial Analyst)** — investment-focused, not typical for tax specialists.
- **Certified Tax Planner** — various providers; less rigorous than CPA.
- **Specializations**: IRS Certified Social Security Expert (not official but various courses available), IRMAA expert training (often bundled with retirement income planning).

Best candidates often have:
- CPA license (required for credibility and return preparation)
- 8–15 years experience in retirement tax planning (not just compliance)
- Advanced training in Social Security taxation and IRMAA
- Track record of producing multi-year projections and Roth conversion analyses
- Experience with couples and spousal coordination
- Knowledge of state tax implications (critical if client relocates)

### Market costs

- **Comprehensive retirement tax plan (multi-year projections, Roth analysis, capital gains strategy)**: $2,500–$6,000 (15–30 hours). High-end ($6k) for complex couple situations with investments.
- **Hourly consulting**: $200–$400/hour depending on CPA credential and market. Solo practitioners and boutique firms at high end.
- **Tax return preparation only** (no strategy): $1,500–$3,000 for complex return (sole proprietor, investment income, multiple states).
- **Capital gains harvest analysis**: $500–$1,500 for one-time review and recommendations.

### Vetting questions for candidates

1. **IRMAA coordination**: "A client's provisional income is $190k. They're deciding between Social Security at 67 (which adds $25k to provisional income) or delaying to 70. How do you model the IRMAA impact on Medicare premiums? Walk me through the trade-off."

2. **Multi-year projections**: "What software or method do you use to build a 20-year tax projection for a retired couple? How do you handle variables like market return, Social Security COLA, RMD changes?"

3. **Roth conversion**: "A 65-year-old client has $50k AGI before a $40k Roth conversion. Federal tax rate is 12%. How do you analyze whether this conversion is worth it, and what long-term benefit analysis do you show?"

4. **Capital gains harvesting**: "A client has a $150k appreciation in one holding and a $20k loss in another. You want to harvest the loss and reinvest. How do you handle wash-sale rules, and what's your documentation process?"

5. **Iowa tax advantage**: "Your client is moving to Iowa. What's the tax advantage or disadvantage compared to their prior state? How does this affect Social Security claiming strategy?"

6. **Coordination with Retirement Specialist**: "You're working with a Retirement Specialist on the same client. They model Social Security claiming at 67 vs 70. You model the tax impact. How do you communicate findings and coordinate recommendations?"

7. **Quarterly estimated taxes**: "Your client has $100k in Social Security income plus $40k in 401k withdrawals. What's your approach to estimated tax planning? Any safe harbor strategies you use?"

8. **References**: "Can you share a case study where tax planning changed the client's retirement outcome? What was the most significant insight from your analysis?"
